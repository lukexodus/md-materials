# COBIT

---

## 1. What Is COBIT?

**COBIT** stands for **Control Objectives for Information and Related Technologies.**

- Developed by: **ISACA**
- First released: **1996**
- Current version: **COBIT 2019**
- Purpose: A **framework for IT governance and management**
- Used by: Enterprises, auditors, regulators, IT leaders globally

COBIT answers a fundamental question:
> *How does an organization govern and manage its information and technology to create value while managing risk?*

## 2. Evolution of COBIT

| Version | Year | Key Change |
|---|---|---|
| COBIT 1 | 1996 | Audit-focused, 34 high-level control objectives |
| COBIT 2 | 1998 | Added control practices |
| COBIT 3 | 2000 | Management guidelines added |
| COBIT 4.0/4.1 | 2005/2007 | Maturity models, IT governance focus |
| COBIT 5 | 2012 | Unified framework, 5 principles, enablers |
| COBIT 2019 | 2018 | Design factors, flexible governance, updated principles |

## 3. COBIT 2019 Core Principles

COBIT 2019 is built on **6 principles for a governance system** and **3 principles for a governance framework.**

---

### Principles for a Governance System:

| #   | Principle                               | Meaning                                                           |
| --- | --------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Value for Stakeholders**              | Every enterprise exists to create value for stakeholders          |
| 2   | **Holistic Approach**                   | Governance requires a complete set of components working together |
| 3   | **Dynamic Governance System**           | The system must adapt as conditions change                        |
| 4   | **Governance Distinct from Management** | Governance and management are different activities                |
| 5   | **Tailored to Enterprise Needs**        | No one-size-fits-all; customize to context                        |
| 6   | **End-to-End Governance System**        | Covers the entire enterprise, not just IT department              |

---

### Principles for a Governance Framework:

| #   | Principle                        | Meaning                                                    |
| --- | -------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Based on Conceptual Model**    | Consistent and complete reference model                    |
| 2   | **Open and Flexible**            | Can incorporate new content without breaking the framework |
| 3   | **Aligned with Major Standards** | Aligns with ISO, ITIL, NIST, PMBOK, etc.                   |

## 4. Governance vs. Management in COBIT

This distinction is **central** to COBIT.

| Aspect | Governance | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Question answered | Are we doing the right things? | Are we doing them well? |
| Who does it | Board, executives | Management, IT leaders |
| COBIT domain | **EDM** | **APO, BAI, DSS, MEA** |
| Focus | Direction, oversight, accountability | Planning, building, running, monitoring |
| Analogy | Board of directors | CEO and operational team |

This is the **governance domain** — handled at the board/executive level.

## 6. COBIT Performance Management

Each of the 40 objectives has a **capability level** from 0 to 5.

| Level | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | **Incomplete** | Process not implemented or fails its purpose |
| 1 | **Performed** | Process achieves its purpose |
| 2 | **Managed** | Process is planned, monitored, adjusted |
| 3 | **Established** | Process follows a defined standard |
| 4 | **Predictable** | Process operates within defined limits statistically |
| 5 | **Optimizing** | Process continuously improves |

This is based on **ISO/IEC 33000** (formerly SPICE/ISO 15504).

## 7. COBIT Design Factors

COBIT 2019 introduced **Design Factors** — contextual variables that shape how you tailor the framework to your organization.

| Design Factor | Examples |
|---|---|
| Enterprise Strategy | Growth, innovation, cost leadership, compliance |
| Enterprise Goals | Financial, customer, internal, learning |
| Risk Profile | Low, medium, high risk tolerance |
| I&T-Related Issues | Current pain points in IT |
| Threat Landscape | Low to very high threat environment |
| Compliance Requirements | Regulatory, legal, contractual |
| Role of IT | Support, factory, turnaround, strategic |
| Sourcing Model | Outsourced, in-house, cloud, hybrid |
| IT Implementation Methods | Agile, DevOps, traditional |
| Technology Adoption Strategy | First mover, follower, slow adopter |
| Enterprise Size | Small, medium, large |

These factors determine:
- Which objectives matter most
- What capability level to target
- How to prioritize investment

## 9. COBIT Goals Cascade

One of COBIT's most powerful concepts — connecting business needs all the way down to IT processes.

```
Stakeholder Needs
        ↓
Enterprise Goals (aligned to Balanced Scorecard)
        ↓
Alignment Goals (I&T-related goals)
        ↓
Governance & Management Objectives
        ↓
Process Activities
```

### Enterprise Goals (Examples):
| Perspective | Goal Example |
|---|---|
| Financial | Managed business risk |
| Customer | Customer-oriented service culture |
| Internal | Optimization of business process functionality |
| Learning & Growth | Skilled and motivated workforce |

### Alignment Goals (Examples):
| # | Alignment Goal |
|---|---|
| AG01 | I&T risk managed to acceptable levels |
| AG02 | Benefits realized from I&T investments |
| AG03 | Realized benefits from digital transformation |

## 10. COBIT Components of a Governance System

COBIT 2019 identifies **7 components** that together make up a governance system:

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Processes** | The 40 governance/management objectives |
| **Organizational Structures** | Decision-making bodies (board, committees, roles) |
| **Principles, Policies, Frameworks** | Rules and guidelines |
| **Information** | Data needed to operate governance |
| **Culture, Ethics, Behavior** | Individual and organizational behavior |
| **People, Skills, Competencies** | Human capability required |
| **Services, Infrastructure, Applications** | Technology enabling governance |

All 7 components must work together — weakness in one affects the whole system.

## 11. COBIT and RACI Charts

Each COBIT process includes a **RACI chart** defining roles:

| Letter | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **R** | Responsible – does the work |
| **A** | Accountable – owns the outcome |
| **C** | Consulted – provides input |
| **I** | Informed – kept up to date |

Common roles in COBIT RACI:
- Board
- CEO / CFO / COO
- CIO / CISO
- IT Steering Committee
- Business Process Owner
- Head of IT Operations
- Head of IT Architecture
- Head of Development
- Service Manager
- Risk Officer
- Compliance Officer
- Audit

| Framework | How COBIT Aligns |
|---|---|

| **ISO 27001** | Security standard; maps to COBIT APO13 and DSS05 |
| **NIST CSF** | Cybersecurity framework; aligns with COBIT cybersecurity focus area |
| **ISO 38500** | IT governance standard; COBIT operationalizes its principles |

| **TOGAF** | Enterprise architecture; aligns with COBIT APO03 |
| **SOX** | Financial regulation; COBIT used for IT controls in SOX compliance |

## 14. Implementing COBIT – Practical Steps

ISACA recommends a **7-phase implementation approach:**

| Phase | Name | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | What are the drivers? | Identify business pain points and triggers |
| 2 | Where are we now? | Assess current state (capability levels) |
| 3 | Where do we want to be? | Define target capability levels |
| 4 | What needs to be done? | Gap analysis and improvement roadmap |
| 5 | How do we get there? | Execute improvement initiatives |
| 6 | Did we get there? | Measure and monitor outcomes |
| 7 | How do we keep the momentum? | Sustain and embed into culture |

This cycle is **continuous** — not a one-time project.

## 15. COBIT Assessment Program

Organizations can formally assess their COBIT capability using:

- **COBIT Self-Assessment** – internal evaluation
- **Independent Assessment** – third-party review
- **COBIT Assessor Certification** – for professionals conducting assessments

Assessment produces:
- Capability level ratings per objective
- Gap identification
- Improvement recommendations

## 16. Criticisms of COBIT

- Can be **complex and overwhelming** for smaller organizations
- Requires significant **training and expertise** to implement properly
- Risk of **checkbox compliance** rather than genuine governance improvement
- High cost of **official COBIT publications and training**
- [Inference] Organizations may adopt COBIT terminology without actually improving governance practices — *outcomes are not guaranteed*
